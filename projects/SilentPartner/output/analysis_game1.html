SUMMARY

The game ends with an unambiguous liberal win — Dom's conviction that Rachel was Hitler was correct, and he executed it cleanly. But the more analytically interesting question is the identity of Rachel's fascist partner, since the final reveal only confirmed Hitler, leaving the table never formally naming the second traitor.

The dominant hypothesis is that John is the non-Hitler fascist, and the case is strong. The "right one" slip [1248s] is the most telling moment in the entire game: when you have two cards in hand and your job as a liberal is simply to play the non-fascist, there is no such thing as "the right one" — you just play the liberal. The framing only makes sense if you know which card serves your team. John then enacted the fascist despite holding a liberal card, a choice Rachel confirmed was available to him [1270s]. This alone would be damning circumstantial evidence; combined with the slip, it reads like a player who momentarily forgot to hide their reasoning. His aggressive verbal liberalism throughout — protests, accusations, volunteered information — fits the pattern of a fascist who compensates for bad policy choices with performative innocence.

Charlotte remains the most credible alternative suspect. Her presidential round sent 2 fascists directly to Rachel at the game's most critical juncture [944-960s], a pipeline that would have been trivially easy for the fascist to engineer. The Charlotte-John mutual accusation that dominated the game's second half is a classic ambiguity — one of them is a fascist deflecting onto an innocent liberal. The argument for Charlotte-as-fascist hinges on whether her "I don't know who Hitler is" statements [1423s] were deliberate lies. The argument against it is the kill-threat directed at John [1675s], which is a costly and sustained signal that doesn't fit a fascist protecting their teammate under cover.

The single piece of information that would most change this read is John's card in the "right one" moment. Rachel confirmed she gave him 1 liberal + 1 fascist. The question of whether John enacted the fascist through deliberate choice or through some rationalised liberal reasoning (e.g., "I'll give the fascist to Charlotte to test her") is the crux. But Rachel's Nein vote on her own election [1769s] suggests Rachel knew the jig was up — which means John's cover was already blown by that point, and the fascist team's coordination ultimately unravelled because Dom's read was sharper than their performance.
